Solar Energy Schemes

Solar energy is a renewable source of power that has gained increasing popularity in recent years. There are a variety of solar energy schemes that can be implemented to harness this energy and convert it into usable electricity.

Solar energy is the energy that is derived from the sun and is harnessed through various methods such as photovoltaic (PV) cells, solar thermal collectors, and concentrated solar power (CSP) systems. It is a renewable source of energy that produces no emissions, and its usage is becoming increasingly important in the fight against climate change.

Scheme 1:

PM-KSUM

The Pradhan Mantri Kisan Urja Suraksha evam Utthaan Mahabhiyan (PM-KUSUM) is a scheme launched by the Government of India in 2019. The scheme aims to promote the use of solar energy among farmers in the country. It also seeks to provide them with a sustainable source of income by enabling them to sell surplus solar power generated by them to the grid. The scheme is implemented by the Ministry of New and Renewable Energy (MNRE) in collaboration with state governments and various public sector companies.

Under the PM-KUSUM scheme, farmers are provided with three components - installation of solar pumps, solarization of existing grid-connected agriculture pumps, and the installation of solar power plants. The scheme aims to add a solar capacity of 25,750 MW by 2022 with a total central financial support of Rs. 34,422 crore.


Scheme 2:

Development of solar parks and ultra mega power projects

The Government of India has launched several schemes to promote the development of solar parks and ultra mega solar power projects in the country. One such scheme is the 'Scheme for Development of Solar Parks and Ultra Mega Solar Power Projects'. Under this scheme, the government provides financial assistance to states and their implementing agencies for setting up large-scale solar parks and solar power projects. The aim of the scheme is to reduce the cost of solar power generation by providing economies of scale, reducing risks associated with land acquisition and providing a framework for easy regulatory approvals.

Scheme 3:

National Solar Mission

The National Solar Mission is a government scheme launched in 2010 by the Ministry of New and Renewable Energy (MNRE) with the goal of achieving 100 GW of solar power capacity by 2022. The mission aims to promote the use of solar power in India by providing policy and regulatory frameworks, promoting research and development, and facilitating the development of solar infrastructure. The mission also includes several other objectives, such as creating a favorable environment for the manufacturing of solar equipment, promoting the use of off-grid solar applications, and increasing awareness about the benefits of solar energy.







Wind Energy Schemes

Wind energy is a form of renewable energy that harnesses the power of wind to generate electricity. Wind turbines are used to convert the kinetic energy of wind into electrical energy. These turbines have blades that are connected to a rotor, which spins as wind blows over them. The spinning rotor then turns a generator, which produces electricity.

Wind energy has several advantages over conventional sources of energy. For one, it is a clean source of energy that produces no greenhouse gas emissions or pollution. Wind energy is also abundant and available in many parts of the world, making it a reliable and sustainable source of power. Additionally, wind energy is cost-competitive with other sources of energy, and the cost of wind power has been decreasing rapidly in recent years.

Scheme 1:

MNRE Wind Power Programme

The Ministry of New and Renewable Energy's (MNRE) Wind Power Program is a government scheme that aims to promote the development of wind power in India. The scheme provides financial incentives for the installation of wind turbines, such as generation-based incentives, accelerated depreciation benefits, and concessional customs and excise duty. These incentives have helped to make wind power a cost-effective source of energy in India, and have encouraged the development of large-scale wind power projects across the country.

The Wind Power Program also provides support for research and development in the wind energy sector. The MNRE has established several centers of excellence and test centers for wind energy technology, which provide technical support and testing facilities for wind turbine manufacturers and developers. This has helped to improve the efficiency and reliability of wind turbines, and has helped to reduce the cost of wind power generation.


Scheme 2:

The National Wind-Solar Hybrid Policy

The National Wind-Solar Hybrid Policy is a government scheme launched by the Ministry of New and Renewable Energy (MNRE) to promote the development of hybrid wind and solar power projects in India. The policy aims to enhance the reliability and stability of the grid by combining the complementary nature of wind and solar power. Under the policy, developers are incentivized to set up wind-solar hybrid projects through a range of financial and regulatory benefits, such as accelerated depreciation, lower transmission charges, and flexibility in the use of land.

Scheme 3:

Wind Energy Development Scheme

The Wind Energy Development Scheme is a government scheme launched by the Ministry of New and Renewable Energy (MNRE) to promote the development of large-scale wind energy projects in India. The scheme provides financial support for the development of wind power projects, including onshore and offshore wind farms. Under the scheme, the MNRE provides financial incentives such as capital subsidy and viability gap funding to developers of wind power projects. The scheme also provides support for the development of wind parks, which are clusters of wind turbines that can be developed in suitable areas to harness wind energy efficiently.







Tidal Energy

Tidal energy is a form of renewable energy that is generated by harnessing the power of ocean tides. As the tides are driven by the gravitational pull of the moon and the sun, tidal energy is a reliable and predictable source of renewable energy. There are various technologies used to harness tidal energy, including tidal barrages, tidal turbines, and tidal lagoons.

Tidal barrages are large dams that are built across estuaries or bays. When the tide comes in, the water flows into the barrage, and as the tide goes out, the water is released, spinning turbines that generate electricity. Tidal turbines are similar to wind turbines, but they are submerged underwater and are designed to operate in the strong currents created by tides. Tidal lagoons are artificial basins built along the coast that capture the incoming tides and use them to drive turbines to generate electricity.

Tidal energy has several advantages over other forms of renewable energy. It is a consistent and predictable source of energy, unlike solar and wind energy, which are dependent on weather conditions. Tidal energy also has a higher energy density than wind and solar energy, meaning that it can generate more power from a smaller area. However, tidal energy is still in the early stages of development, and there are challenges associated with the technology, such as high capital costs, environmental impacts, and limited availability of suitable sites for tidal energy projects. Despite these challenges, tidal energy has the potential to play an important role in the renewable energy mix, particularly in coastal regions with high tidal ranges.

Geo Thermal Energy

Geothermal energy is a form of renewable energy that is generated by harnessing the natural heat of the earth's interior. The heat is generated by the natural decay of radioactive elements in the earth's crust and mantle, and it is transferred to the surface through conduction and convection. Geothermal energy can be used for heating and cooling buildings, generating electricity, and other industrial applications.

The most common method of generating electricity from geothermal energy involves drilling deep wells into hot geothermal reservoirs, which are then used to produce steam. The steam is used to turn turbines and generate electricity, which is then fed into the power grid. In addition to generating electricity, geothermal energy can also be used for direct heating and cooling applications, such as heating homes and buildings.

Geothermal energy is a reliable and predictable source of renewable energy that can provide baseload power, which means it can operate continuously and is not dependent on weather conditions. It is also a clean source of energy that emits minimal greenhouse gases and other pollutants. However, geothermal energy requires specialized technology and infrastructure, and it is only available in certain areas with high geothermal potential.

India has a significant potential for geothermal energy, with estimated reserves of up to 10 GW. The Ministry of New and Renewable Energy (MNRE) has included geothermal energy under its Renewable Energy Program and has initiated various research and development projects to explore the potential of geothermal energy in India. While there are currently no specific government schemes in India exclusively focused on geothermal energy, the MNRE has implemented various initiatives and schemes to support the development of renewable energy, which could indirectly benefit the development of geothermal energy projects.
